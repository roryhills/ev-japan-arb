Gamma Scalping:
Generating Returns in Any Market Direction

How active delta hedging converts two-way stock price movement into realised gains — illustrated across three contrasting months of the Taiyo Yuden position. The fund made positive returns in all three despite materially different stock price environments.

Position: Taiyo Yuden (6976 JP)
Period shown: Three selected months
Methodology: LIFO · Actual trade data

The Mechanic — Four Steps
01
Stock rises

The ASCOT's delta increases. The portfolio manager sells additional equity short to rebalance the hedge — locking in short exposure at higher prices.

02
Stock falls back

Delta decreases. The manager covers part of the short — buying equity back at lower prices. The spread is locked in as a realised gain.

03
Repeat

Each oscillation triggering a hedge adjustment generates a small realised gain. Over a month with multiple moves, these can accumulate into a meaningful contribution.

04
Direction irrelevant

The process works in rising, falling, and sideways markets. Two-way movement is the requirement — not net direction over the month.

Delta: mathematical calculation, discretionary execution
The delta of an ASCOT at any point — the theoretical sensitivity of the instrument's price to movements in the underlying stock — is primarily a mathematical output derived from a pricing model. What is discretionary is how and when the manager acts on it: whether to hedge to exactly 100% of theoretical delta or leave a small residual long; how large a move to wait for before adjusting; and how to account for the real-world behaviour of the instrument which, particularly when deeply in-the-money, can differ from what a model predicts. This is where 30 years of actually trading these instruments — rather than reading about them — makes the difference.
Why the manager maintains a small residual long exposure
As stocks rally, borrow costs on the short equity position and financing costs of the long CB escalate — the underhedged position partially compensates for this. More fundamentally, CB valuations and implied volatilities tend to cheapen as stocks rise, meaning the ASCOT may trade below what a theoretical model would suggest. A small net long reflects this real-world dynamic. As Chris McGuire, the portfolio manager, puts it: "Nothing to do with any sort of 'we think markets go up' — much more to do with many years' experience telling you that the mechanics of the instruments means you want to be slightly underhedged relative to what the maths models or textbooks might tell you. Especially when the CB is well in the money."

Why April 2025 generated such an outsized return
April 2025 saw a tariff-driven shock that sent Taiyo Yuden's stock down 30.2% in the first week of the month — from an opening price of ¥2,490.5 to a low of ¥1,739.5 — before recovering 22.2% off that low to close the month down 13.4%. The full intra-month range was −31.7% (high to low). The stock's annualised realised volatility for the month reached 107.8% — roughly double the stock's realised volatility over the position's life to date (~60%). This extreme two-way oscillation at large notional sizes generated the outsized scalping result: the fund sold short heavily into the initial collapse, covered on bounces, and repeated through a series of violent intra-month swings. The month-end return (−13.4%) materially understates what actually happened — it was the violence and range of the oscillation, not the net direction, that drove returns.
Why this vol edge exists — and why it is structural, not accidental
When Chris entered the Taiyo Yuden ASCOT, the implied volatility embedded in the CB's pricing was approximately 35-36%. Over the life of the position, the stock has subsequently realised an annualised volatility of approximately 60% — nearly double what was priced in. The option was purchased cheaply, and the stock moved far more than the market expected.

This is not an accident of timing. When a company issues a CB, its primary concerns are the coupon (zero coupon financing is extremely attractive to Japanese corporates) and the conversion premium (its impact on potential dilution). The implied volatility is a secondary consideration — it is whatever the market will accept to clear the deal at the desired coupon and premium. This is fundamentally different from bank-issued options, where the bank must price IV above expected realised volatility to hedge its own risk. The corporate issuer has no such constraint. The result: CB-embedded options are structurally cheap relative to subsequently realised volatility. The strategy does not need volatility to rise; it simply needs realised volatility to remain broadly where it is. If it rises further, so much the better. And given the margin of cheapness (35% paid vs 60% realised for Taiyo Yuden), there is meaningful room for vol to fall before the edge disappears.

Convexity:
Why the Upside and Downside Are Not Equal

How the convertible bond structure creates an asymmetric payoff — and how the ASCOT amplifies this further.

Conversion price: ¥4,360
Entry (Oct 2024): ¥2,934
Current (Jun 2026): ¥16,630+
Intraday high (Jun 2026): ¥22,000

Convexity describes the way an instrument's sensitivity to price changes varies depending on where it trades relative to its reference price. For a convertible bond, this creates a positively skewed payoff: when the stock falls, the CB falls less (the bond floor provides a brake); when the stock rises, the CB participates strongly. This asymmetry is structural — embedded in the instrument itself. It is most pronounced when the position is close to at-the-money, and compresses as the position moves deep in-the-money.

What is an ASCOT — and how does it relate to the CB?
The fund does not hold the full convertible bond. It holds an ASCOT (Asset Swapped Convertible Option Transaction) — the equity-option-like component of the CB, stripped of its credit/bond element. The ASCOT's value is simply the CB price less an implied bond floor (the credit component). This means the ASCOT captures all of the CB's equity-linked appreciation, from a very small starting base — generating far greater percentage leverage than the CB itself. The ASCOT holder retains the right to reconstitute the full CB at any time, which is the structural underpinning of the synthetic put described in the next section. Notably, the bond floor has remained stable at ~91-94% of par throughout the position despite 5-year JGB yields rising from ~0.75% to ~1.9% since the fund launched — which might ordinarily have pressured bond prices. The stability likely reflects the further strengthening of the company's credit standing as the stock has risen, partly offsetting the rate move.

May 2026 to today · deep Equity-like
The stock accelerated sharply, reaching ¥11,445 at the point our data was compiled — at which level the CB was trading at 262% of par, representing a 2.5× gain on the CB since entry. Since then, the stock has risen further still, touching an intraday high of ¥22,000 in June 2026 — almost double the ¥11,445 level and nearly 5× the ¥4,360 conversion price. That further move has driven significant additional convexity gains to the portfolio. The position is now 100% hedged and held purely as a synthetic put (see next section).
The ASCOT's leverage in numbers
The fund entered the Taiyo Yuden ASCOT in October 2024 at a value of approximately 12.8 (% of par). By June 2026, with the stock having risen from ¥2,934 to ¥16,630, the CB price had risen from ~103% to ~262% of par — a 2.5× increase. But because the bond floor remained nearly constant at ~91-94%, the ASCOT (which is CB minus floor) rose from ~12.8 to ~168: a 13× increase on the position Chris actually holds. This is convexity in practice: structural leverage embedded in the instrument, requiring no additional risk or capital to access.
The discretionary hedging dynamic — and the give-back question
As the stock moves, the portfolio manager adjusts the equity hedge to manage delta exposure. The fund typically maintains a small residual net long (see Gamma Scalping section for the rationale). In periods of strong appreciation, this amplifies gains. If the stock then reverses before an additional hedge is added, some of those gains may be given back. Give-back can only occur where there were prior gains to give back. The manager can also choose to fully hedge at any point — not a month-end decision, but a live choice executable at any minute of any trading day.

The Synthetic Put:
Capital-Efficient Optionality on a Sharp Reversal

When a position is so deeply in-the-money that the ASCOT and stock trade as one, it takes on a third role: a highly cash-efficient "synthetic put" — capable of generating outsized, negatively correlated gains if the stock falls sharply.

Conversion price: ¥4,360
Stock (Jun 2026): ¥16,630
Intraday high (Jun 2026): ¥22,000
Current hedge ratio: ~100%
The third purpose
When a position is very deep in-the-money, as Taiyo Yuden is today, the delta is 1, and the ASCOT and stock should trade identically; as such, there is little or no scope for gamma trading, nor gains from the convexity of the ASCOT, but — at this point — the position serves a third purpose, as a very cash-efficient "synthetic put". Cash efficient because, with the position 100% hedged, it is extremely low cost to hold, but should the stock price fall dramatically — either for market or idiosyncratic reasons — there will come a point where the ASCOT's convexity reasserts itself, generating a potential outsized, and very negatively correlated, gain.
↗
Stock rallies further

The ASCOT and stock continue to move together. Net P&L near zero. The position remains dormant but intact — the synthetic put is preserved at no cost.

→
Stock moves sideways

The ASCOT and stock continue to move together. Position remains near-costless to hold. Optionality preserved — no time decay, nothing paid to maintain it.

↘
Stock falls sharply

If the fall is large enough, the ASCOT's convexity reasserts and the gap to the stock reopens — generating positive P&L, initially unrealised, then locked in as the short hedge is reduced.

A note on exit flexibility
Once a position is this deep in-the-money, the ASCOT trades essentially like the underlying equity — and there are typically no natural buyers for an instrument behaving this way. This does not constrain the fund: the ASCOT holder retains the right to reconstitute the full convertible bond and convert it into equity at any time. If Chris wished to exit, he would reconstitute, convert, and sell the resulting shares in the equity market — straightforward in practice, and entirely on the fund's own timeline.

Footnotes

FN1 — What "Gamma Scalping" Means. The fund holds an ASCOT on the Taiyo Yuden convertible bond, giving it positive gamma. To remain broadly market-neutral, the manager continuously adjusts a short equity hedge. Each completed sell-high / buy-low round-trip locks in a realised gain. This works regardless of whether the stock ultimately goes up or down — what matters is that the stock oscillates.

FN2 — P&L Decomposition. Eagle's View Japan Arb's risk system (TS Imagine) produces total P&L at the position level only, encompassing the ASCOT, equity hedge, and all associated trades. It does not separately report gamma scalping, convexity, unrealised gains, changes in implied volatility, or other sub-components. The gamma scalping analysis for the three illustrative months is independently calculated using actual equity trade data and the LIFO methodology.

FN3 — Realised P&L Calculation (LIFO). Gamma scalping gains are calculated using LIFO matching of equity trades. Each buy-to-cover is matched against the most recently established short position of equivalent size.

FN4 — Base Hedge Exclusion. When a new ASCOT or CB tranche is purchased, the manager simultaneously sells shares short to establish delta neutrality. These initial shorts are the structural hedge leg and are excluded from the scalping P&L calculation.

FN5 — VWAP Aggregation & Base Hedge Trades. Multiple sequential same-direction trades on the same day are shown as a single VWAP transaction for display clarity. P&L calculations use underlying individual trade prices. Base hedge trades (initial delta hedges established on ASCOT purchase dates) are included in the trade log for completeness and clearly flagged, but are excluded from all gamma scalping P&L calculations. They represent position-sizing activity rather than gamma scalping and are neutral to the analysis.

FN6 — Basis Point Contribution. Monthly bp contribution = monthly scalping gain (USD) ÷ fund AUM at month start.

FN7 — Realised Volatility & Range. Monthly realised volatility = annualised standard deviation of daily log returns within the month, calculated as daily std dev × √252. Intra-month range = (month low − month open) ÷ month open.

FN8 — Currency. All P&L in USD. Stock and CB prices in JPY. Monthly average USD/JPY rates used for conversion where applicable.

FN9 — Gross Figures. All P&L gross of management fees (2%), performance allocation (20%), stock borrow costs, and financing costs.

FN10 — Single Position. This analysis covers Taiyo Yuden within a portfolio where 25–30 positions is broadly representative of the average number held since inception; the portfolio launched with 8–10 positions and has grown to approximately 40 today. Growth was not linear — positions moved to around 20 relatively quickly before growing more gradually to current levels. Taiyo Yuden is used here as an illustrative example of the strategy's mechanics and should not be taken as representative of all positions or overall fund performance.

FN11 — Fund Returns. Total return since inception and annualised return are calculated from monthly net returns as reported in the fund's tearsheet, covering September 2024 (inception) through May 2026 (estimated). Past performance is not indicative of future results.

FN13 — Reconstitution Right. The ASCOT holder retains the right to reconstitute the underlying convertible bond at any time and convert it into the underlying equity. This right is a structural feature of the ASCOT instrument in Japan and underpins both the convexity behaviour described in this document and the exit flexibility described in the Synthetic Put section.

FN14 — ASCOT Tranches. The fund holds ASCOTs rather than warrants — the ASCOT itself is the option component. As fund AUM grows or position weightings are adjusted, new ASCOTs are created for the same underlying convertible bond. Since each tranche references the same CB with the same credit, terms and maturity, all tranches will carry identical or near-identical prices at any given time, regardless of when they were established.
